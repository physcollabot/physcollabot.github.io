Overview

Why this workshop, and why now?

Robots are moving beyond controlled laboratory setups toward collaborative construction, cooperative manufacturing, and embodied logistics. In those settings, many tasks require more than coordination: they require teams of robots to couple through objects, contacts, shared loads, and the environment itself.

Most multi-robot research still assumes loosely coupled planning problems where agents can be optimized largely independently. PhysCollaBot focuses on the opposite regime. We want to highlight methods that reason jointly about force, contact, task dependencies, and execution constraints, while bridging model-based structure with the scalability of learning-based systems.

The goal is not just to present recent results. The goal is to help define the next research frontier for deploying multi-robot systems that must act together in the physical world.

What the workshop will do

  • Bridge control, planning, task and motion planning, and robot learning communities.
  • Surface practical abstractions for contact-rich, tightly coupled collaboration.
  • Discuss long-horizon dependencies in construction, assembly, and shared manipulation.
  • Connect recent single-robot embodied intelligence advances to multi-robot settings.

Core Questions

Three challenges define the agenda

The workshop is organized around the bottlenecks that repeatedly show up when multi-robot systems move from abstract coordination to physical collaboration.

Reasoning over physical interaction

Teams must reason about contact, load sharing, and mutual constraints without collapsing into models that are too coarse for deployment or too expensive for planning.

Long-horizon task dependencies

Collective transport, construction, and assembly require temporal reasoning, resource coupling, and execution-aware planning over large makespans.

Scaling embodied intelligence to teams

Data-efficient methods, role allocation, and coordinated adaptation remain open problems when capabilities learned for one robot must scale to multi-robot collaboration.
