Workshop Highlight

When multiple robots coordinate through physical interaction

The workshop focuses on tasks where robots are coupled through shared physical interactions with objects, the environment, or each other, such that the motions or forces of one robot constrain the actions of the others.

Workshop Theme

Physical collaboration as a robotics frontier

As robots increasingly move beyond controlled laboratory settings, emerging applications—such as collaborative construction and cooperative transportation—require capabilities that exceed those of a single robot. These tasks naturally motivate the use of multiple robots that must physically coordinate their actions.

Most existing multi-robot research has focused on loosely coupled coordination problems, where robots can be planned largely independently. PhysCollaBot addresses the opposite regime—physically collaborative tasks that require robots to reason jointly about contact interactions, shared physical constraints, and long-horizon task dependencies—while bridging model-based and learning-based coordination.

Through invited talks, peer-reviewed contributions, and panel discussions, the workshop seeks to help define the next research frontier for real-world multi-robot collaboration.

What to expect

  • Bridge control, motion planning, task and motion planning, and robot learning communities.
  • Identify new abstractions, algorithmic principles, and learning paradigms for tightly coupled collaboration.
  • Address long-horizon task dependencies in collaborative transportation, construction, and manufacturing.
  • Integrate single-robot intelligence into multi-robot systems.

Core Questions

Three challenges define the agenda

We outline several key challenges that currently limit the development of methods for tightly coupled physical collaboration.

Efficient reasoning models for physical interaction

Robots must reason about complex physical interactions with the environment and with one another, yet overly simplified models fail in practice while high-fidelity models are too expensive for planning and control.

Long-horizon task dependencies

Real-world tasks require reasoning about temporal dependencies, shared resources, and coupled motion constraints. Existing approaches often suffer from the curse of dimensionality or simplify task structure, limiting their ability to handle rich, interdependent objectives.

Scaling single-robot intelligence to multi-robot collaboration

Coordinated demonstrations are costly to obtain, making training data scarce for multi-robot teams. Meanwhile, teams must handle far greater task diversity, requiring coordination, role assignment, and adaptation to changing environments.
